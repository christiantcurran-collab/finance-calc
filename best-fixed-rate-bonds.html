What are Fixed Rate Bonds?

Fixed rate bonds (also known as fixed term deposits) are savings accounts where you lock your money away for a set period in exchange for a guaranteed interest rate. They typically offer higher rates than easy access savings accounts but your money is inaccessible for the full term.

Things to Consider

Lock-in Period

Your money is locked away for the full term. Early withdrawal usually means losing interest or paying significant penalties.

FSCS Protection

Ensure the account is covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) up to £85,000 per person, per institution.

Interest Payment Options

Choose between monthly, annual, or maturity payments. Annual payments offer compound growth if reinvested.

Tax on Interest

Interest is taxable but most people have a Personal Savings Allowance (£1,000 for basic rate, £500 for higher rate taxpayers).

Rate Comparison

Longer terms don't always mean better rates. Compare across different terms to find the best deal for your needs.

Maturity Options

When your bond matures, you typically have a short window (14-30 days) to reinvest or withdraw without penalty.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are fixed rate bonds safe?

Yes, as long as they're covered by the FSCS. This protects up to £85,000 per person, per financial institution if the provider goes bust.

Can I access my money early?

Most fixed rate bonds don't allow early access without significant penalties. If you think you might need access, consider easy access savings or notice accounts instead.

What happens when my bond matures?

When your bond matures, the provider will usually transfer your money (plus interest) to an easy access account. You'll typically have 14-30 days to reinvest at a new rate or withdraw without penalty.

Should I choose a longer term for a higher rate?

Not necessarily. Longer terms don't always offer better rates in the current market. Consider your financial goals, whether you might need access to the money, and whether rates might rise or fall.

How is the interest paid?

Depends on the provider and product. Options include: monthly (good for income), annually (allows compound growth), or at maturity (simplest option). Check the AER (Annual Equivalent Rate) to compare fairly.
